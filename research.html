Published & Forthcoming Papers

Recent Advances of Deep Learning in Bioinformatics and Computational Biology
with Binhua Tang, Zixiang Pan and Asif Khateeb.
Front. Genet. , 26 March 2019.

Extracting inherent valuable knowledge from omics big data remains as a daunting problem in bioinformatics and computational biology. Deep learning, as an emerging branch from machine learning, has exhibited unprecedented performance in quite a few applications from academia and industry. We highlight the difference and similarity in widely utilized models in deep learning studies, through discussing their basic structures, and reviewing diverse applications and disadvantages. We anticipate the work can serve as a meaningful perspective for further development of its theory, algorithm and application in bioinformatic and computational biology.

Smartphone based oblique incidence reflectometer
with Yuan Cao.

The aggressive advancements of semiconductor technology had pervaded smartphones into every corner of this world and along with it, the state of art complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS) enabled cameras. In this letter, we present a smartphone based oblique incidence reflectometer (OIR) that utilizes its built-in camera for imaging the diffused reflectance light of an incident laser beam and exploit furthermore its computational power to calculate the reduced scattering coefficient of the probed matter. A mobile application integrates the two components into a holistic smartphone based solution with minimal extra accessories. Phantom experiments demonstrated a measurement accuracy of 10% with the smartphone based OIR.© 2014 Optical Society of America.

A Low Cost CMOS Image Sensor with In-field Hot Pixel Detection and Correction Functions for Harsh Environment Applications
with Yuan Cao.

In this paper, we present a novel CMOS image sensor architecture for detecting and correcting the malfunctioned pixels, which are also known as hot pixels. Featuring early saturation of the sensed analog signal that represents the light intensity, the hot pixels always appear bright and are continuously generated in the harsh environment applications, including the space imaging, nuclear monitoring and biomedical imaging, etc. The proposed in-field detection and correction first avoids the replacement of the degenerated image sensors, which is expensive and usually impractical. In addition, compared with the previously reported in-field implementations where the correction filter is applied to the entire image, the proposed correction scheme is quite selective to the hot pixels, leading to well-preserved sharpness of the original raw image. Moreover, the need of additional memory and calibration techniques in the previous implementations for recording/updating the hot pixels’ locations is completely removed. Reported simulation results validate the concept of a low-cost monolithic image sensor integrating both the detection and correction modules together on a single chip.
